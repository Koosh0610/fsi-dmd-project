Dynamic Mode Decomposition of
Cylinder Wake Vortex Shedding

FSI Project — Re = 100 Cylinder Flow Analysis
Abstract — We apply Dynamic Mode Decomposition to 151 vorticity snapshots from a direct numerical simulation of 2D flow past a circular cylinder at $\mathrm{Re} = 100$. Beginning from raw data exploration, we systematically build a spectral picture of the wake: selecting the SVD truncation rank, extracting frequency-ranked coherent structures, validating the recovered Strouhal number ($\mathrm{St} = 0.1654$, within 0.4% of the Williamson 1996 reference), and demonstrating reconstruction fidelity of 99.92%. We decompose the vortex street by harmonic group, analyse centreline wake physics through spectral and spatiotemporal methods, compare DMD with POD, and assess robustness via rank sensitivity sweeps and Bagging Optimised DMD. The 28 figures in this report trace a complete analytical arc from first look at the data to validated, physically interpreted results.
Keywords: Dynamic Mode Decomposition, vortex shedding, Strouhal number, cylinder wake, POD, BOPDMD, Re = 100

§1 Introduction

At $\mathrm{Re} = 100$, the flow past a circular cylinder produces one of the cleanest periodic phenomena in fluid dynamics: a two-dimensional von Kármán vortex street. Alternating vortices detach from the upper and lower surfaces of the cylinder, forming a regular, time-periodic wake. The frequency of this shedding, non-dimensionalised as the Strouhal number $\mathrm{St} = fD/U$, has been measured extensively — Williamson (1996) gives $\mathrm{St} \approx 0.166$ at this Reynolds number.

This makes cylinder wake an ideal test bed for Dynamic Mode Decomposition. DMD, introduced by Schmid (2010) and formalised by Tu et al. (2014), extracts coherent structures that each oscillate at a single frequency. Unlike POD, which ranks modes by energy content, DMD provides a spectral decomposition — each mode has a well-defined frequency and growth rate. For a flow dominated by a single shedding frequency and its harmonics, DMD should produce a clean, interpretable decomposition.

The question driving this report is not just "does DMD work?" but "what can we learn by systematically peeling apart the layers?" We begin with the raw data, build intuition for its structure, select an appropriate rank, fit DMD, and then use the decomposition as an analytical tool: isolating individual harmonics, probing wake physics, comparing with POD, and stress-testing robustness. Each step motivates the next.

§2 Exploring the Data

Before decomposing anything, we need to understand what we are looking at. The dataset consists of 151 snapshots of the vorticity field $\zeta(x, y, t)$ on a $449 \times 199$ grid (89,351 spatial points), sampled at intervals of $\Delta t = 0.2$ non-dimensional time units. The total observation window of $T = 30$ covers roughly five complete shedding cycles.

Figure 1 shows six snapshots spread across the time series. The alternating red and blue patches are positive and negative vortices convecting downstream — the vortex street is clearly periodic. The last snapshot is visually phase-aligned with the first, already hinting at the limit-cycle nature of this flow.

Figure 1. Instantaneous vorticity at six time instants. The periodic vortex street is evident.

To separate the steady and unsteady parts of the flow, we compute the time-averaged fields. The mean vorticity (Fig. 2) shows the antisymmetric shear layers originating from the cylinder surface. The mean streamwise velocity (Fig. 3) reveals the wake deficit — a region of reduced velocity extending several diameters downstream. Together, these define the base flow on which the periodic oscillation is superimposed.

Figure 2. Time-averaged vorticity.
Figure 3. Time-averaged streamwise velocity.

Where in the domain is the flow actually fluctuating? Figure 4 shows the root-mean-square vorticity — the standard deviation at each grid point across all snapshots. The fluctuation intensity concentrates in two lobes straddling the centreline, corresponding to the corridors through which vortices pass. This tells us that a data-driven decomposition must primarily explain the dynamics in this near-wake region.

Figure 4. RMS vorticity fluctuation. The two lobes mark the vortex passage corridors.

§3 SVD and Rank Selection

DMD works by projecting the dynamics onto a low-rank subspace via the truncated SVD. Choosing the truncation rank $r$ is the single most consequential modelling decision: too low, and we lose physical content; too high, and we overfit to noise. The singular value spectrum tells us the intrinsic dimensionality of the data.

For a periodic flow, the spectrum has a characteristic structure: pairs of nearly equal singular values corresponding to the sine and cosine components of each harmonic frequency. Figure 5 shows this clearly — the spectrum descends in a staircase of pairs through the first ~20 modes, then drops sharply into the noise floor.

Figure 5. Singular values (log scale) and cumulative energy. The dashed line marks $r = 21$.

We select $r = 21$: ten harmonic pairs plus one mean-flow mode, capturing 99.91% of the total energy. This is deliberately generous — we want the DMD to have access to at least five harmonics of the fundamental frequency without being contaminated by the noise floor.

§4 DMD Fitting

With the rank chosen, we fit exact DMD with optimised amplitudes (Tu et al. 2014). The algorithm seeks the best-fit linear operator $\mathbf{A}$ satisfying $\mathbf{x}_{k+1} \approx \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}_k$, projected onto the rank-$r$ POD subspace:

$$\tilde{\mathbf{A}} = \mathbf{U}_r^* \mathbf{X}' \mathbf{V}_r \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r^{-1}$$

The eigenvalues $\lambda_k$ of $\tilde{\mathbf{A}}$ encode the frequency and growth rate of each mode via $\omega_k = \ln(\lambda_k)/\Delta t$. For a limit-cycle flow, every eigenvalue should sit on the unit circle ($|\lambda_k| = 1$, i.e., zero growth rate).

Eigenvalues and frequency spectrum

Figure 6 confirms exactly this: all 21 eigenvalues lie on or indistinguishably close to the unit circle. The amplitude spectrum (Fig. 7) reveals a clean harmonic structure — a dominant fundamental at $f_1 = 0.165$ and progressively weaker harmonics at $2f_1, 3f_1, \ldots$ These harmonics arise from the nonlinear (advective) self-interaction of the vortex street; a purely linear instability would produce only $f_1$.

Figure 6. Eigenvalues on the unit circle, coloured by amplitude.
Figure 7. Amplitude spectrum with labelled harmonics.
Strouhal validation: The dominant frequency gives $\mathrm{St} = 0.1654$, matching the Williamson (1996) reference of 0.166 to within 0.4%. This is well inside the $\pm 5\%$ tolerance band.

Growth rates

A natural follow-up: if the eigenvalues are near the unit circle, how near? Figure 8 plots the continuous-time growth rate $\mathrm{Re}(\omega_k)$ for each mode. The largest absolute value is $1.4 \times 10^{-4}$ — seven orders of magnitude below the oscillation frequencies. The flow is neutrally stable to within numerical precision, confirming a saturated limit cycle with no transient behaviour in this observation window.

Figure 8. Growth rates are effectively zero — all modes are neutrally stable.

Spatial modes and temporal dynamics

Each DMD mode $\boldsymbol{\phi}_k$ is a spatial field that oscillates at exactly one frequency. Figure 9 shows the six highest-amplitude modes. The dominant pair captures the large-scale vortex street; successive modes show doubled, tripled, and quadrupled spatial wavenumbers — exactly the harmonic progression one expects. This is the spatial fingerprint of a nonlinear periodic flow decomposed into its Fourier-like DMD basis.

Figure 9. First six DMD modes, sorted by amplitude. Spatial wavenumber increases with harmonic order.

The temporal coefficients $b_k \lambda_k^t$ (Fig. 10) confirm the picture: clean sinusoids at the fundamental and harmonic frequencies, with constant amplitude envelopes. The phase relationships between these modes encode the spatial asymmetry of the vortex street — the fact that positive and negative vortices are not mirror images of each other.

Figure 10. Temporal evolution of the four dominant modes.

§5 Reconstruction Quality

A decomposition is only useful if it can faithfully reproduce the original data. The full DMD reconstruction at time step $k$ sums over all modes:

$$\mathbf{x}(k) = \sum_{j=1}^{r} \boldsymbol{\phi}_j \, b_j \, \lambda_j^k$$

Figure 11 compares the true and reconstructed vorticity at snapshot 75. They are visually indistinguishable — the global relative error is just 0.077%.

Figure 11. True (left) vs. DMD reconstruction (right) at snapshot 75. Global error: 0.077%.

But is rank 21 special, or would fewer modes suffice? Figure 12 sweeps the truncation rank from 1 to 81. The error drops steeply as the first few harmonic pairs are added, with visible pairwise steps (each frequency contributes a conjugate pair). Beyond rank ~15, diminishing returns set in. Our choice of $r = 21$ sits comfortably in the converged plateau.

Figure 12. Reconstruction error vs. truncation rank. Pairwise steps reflect the harmonic structure.

§6 Selective Reconstruction and Harmonic Buildup

This is where DMD reveals its real advantage over POD. Because each mode oscillates at a known frequency, we can reconstruct the flow using only modes at specific frequencies. This lets us ask: what does each harmonic physically contribute to the vortex street?

Figure 13 builds up the reconstruction one frequency group at a time. The mean flow alone ($f_0$) gives the steady wake structure with no vortices. Adding the fundamental $f_1$ introduces the alternating vortex pattern. The second harmonic $2f_1$ sharpens the vortex cores and introduces asymmetry between positive and negative vortices. By the time we include through $5f_1$, the result is nearly perfect.

Figure 13. Reconstruction using progressively more frequency groups, from mean-only to full DMD.

Figure 14 shows the same idea as a progressive buildup, with local error reported for each panel. The visual evolution is striking: the fundamental captures the gross pattern, the second harmonic adds the fine structure of the vortex cores, and higher harmonics fill in the thin shear-layer filaments connecting adjacent vortices.

Figure 14. Harmonic buildup with local error. Each added harmonic refines finer spatial scales.

Figure 15 quantifies this: the mean flow alone leaves ~50% error (half the signal is oscillatory), $f_1$ cuts it dramatically, and each subsequent harmonic provides a progressively smaller improvement — consistent with the exponentially decaying amplitude spectrum from Figure 7.

Figure 15. Cumulative error reduction as harmonics are added.
Key insight: The vortex street is almost entirely described by five harmonics of a single fundamental frequency. This extreme spectral compactness — a 89,351-dimensional flow captured by ~11 complex numbers — is the signature of a low-Re limit cycle.

§7 Centreline Wake Physics

Having established the DMD decomposition, we now turn to the spatial structure of the wake itself. The centreline ($y = 0$) is a natural probe: vortices cross it as they are shed alternately from the upper and lower shear layers.

Mean velocity deficit

The time-averaged streamwise velocity along the centreline (Fig. 16) shows the classic wake deficit: a minimum immediately behind the cylinder, recovering gradually toward the freestream. The recovery rate is consistent with laminar far-wake theory, which predicts a $x^{-1/2}$ decay.

Figure 16. Mean centreline velocity deficit as a function of downstream distance.

Time traces and power spectra

Monitoring the vorticity signal at fixed downstream stations (Fig. 17) reveals regular oscillations that persist far downstream, though with diminishing amplitude due to viscous diffusion. Crucially, the frequency does not change with distance — the shedding is a globally synchronised instability, not a local convective phenomenon.

Figure 17. Centreline vorticity at six downstream stations. The frequency is constant everywhere.

The power spectra (Fig. 18) make this quantitative. At every station, the dominant peak sits at $\mathrm{St} \approx 0.165$, with weaker harmonics visible at $2f_1$ and $3f_1$. This is independent confirmation of the DMD result: the same frequencies, extracted by a completely different method (FFT of local time series vs. global matrix decomposition).

Figure 18. Power spectra at five downstream positions. The fundamental peak is consistent everywhere.

Spatiotemporal structure and cross-stream profiles

The spatiotemporal ($x$–$t$) diagram (Fig. 19) provides a striking global view: inclined stripes trace individual vortices convecting downstream. The stripe slope gives the vortex phase speed, and the regular spacing confirms the periodicity. The contrast fading at large $x$ reflects viscous dissipation.

Figure 19. Centreline $x$–$t$ diagram. Inclined stripes trace vortex convection.

The cross-stream RMS profiles (Fig. 20) reveal the lateral structure of the wake. The twin-peaked profiles straddle the centreline, marking the vortex passage corridors identified in Figure 4. The peaks separate and weaken downstream as the wake spreads — the normalised profiles nearly collapse, suggesting approximately self-similar behaviour.

Figure 20. Cross-stream RMS vorticity: 2D map (left) and profiles at five stations (right).

Phase portrait

Finally, we construct a phase portrait (Fig. 21) by plotting the centreline vorticity at a near-wake station ($x = 80$) against a far-wake station ($x = 200$). The closed elliptical orbit is the hallmark of a limit cycle. The ellipticity (rather than a perfect circle) reflects the phase lag between the two stations — a vortex takes a finite time to travel from $x = 80$ to $x = 200$.

Figure 21. Phase portrait: near-wake vs. far-wake. The closed orbit confirms a limit cycle.

§8 POD Comparison

How does the DMD decomposition compare to the more classical Proper Orthogonal Decomposition? POD computes the mean-subtracted SVD and ranks modes by captured energy $\sigma_k^2 / \sum \sigma_j^2$. The modes are spatially orthogonal and energy-optimal, but each mode can contain a mixture of temporal frequencies.

Figure 22 shows the first six POD modes with their energy fractions. The leading pair accounts for most of the fluctuation energy and shows the same large-scale vortex pattern as the DMD fundamental. Subsequent pairs capture higher spatial wavenumbers with rapidly diminishing energy contributions.

Figure 22. First six POD modes with energy percentages.

The side-by-side comparison (Fig. 23) makes the correspondence explicit: the dominant POD mode and the dominant DMD mode are nearly identical spatial fields. For this simple periodic flow, the two decompositions converge. The distinction becomes critical for flows with multiple competing frequencies or broadband turbulence, where POD modes mix frequencies and lose spectral interpretability.

Figure 23. Dominant POD mode (left) vs. dominant DMD mode (right). Nearly identical.

§9 Sensitivity Analysis and BOPDMD

A result is only convincing if it is robust. We test the DMD analysis along two axes: sensitivity to the truncation rank, and comparison with an independent algorithm (BOPDMD).

Strouhal stability across ranks

Figure 24 sweeps the truncation rank from $r = 3$ (barely enough for one harmonic pair plus the mean) to $r = 81$ (more than half the snapshots). The recovered Strouhal number is $\mathrm{St} = 0.1654$ at every single rank. This perfect stability demonstrates that the shedding frequency is an intrinsic feature of the data, not a numerical artefact of a particular rank choice.

Figure 24. Strouhal number is perfectly stable across all tested ranks.

Rank–snapshot interaction

Figure 25 maps the reconstruction error jointly over truncation rank and number of snapshots. The heatmap reveals that at least ~40 snapshots (roughly two shedding cycles) are needed for convergence, and that increasing the rank beyond ~15 provides negligible benefit regardless of snapshot count. Our operating point (rank 21, 151 snapshots) sits deep in the converged region.

Figure 25. Reconstruction error as a function of rank and snapshot count.

BOPDMD comparison

Bagging Optimised DMD (Sashidhar & Kutz 2022) takes a fundamentally different approach: it fits continuous-time eigenvalues $\omega_k$ directly through nonlinear optimisation, using 100 bootstrap subsamples for statistical robustness. We enforce conjugate pairing and purely imaginary eigenvalues, appropriate for a limit cycle.

The eigenvalue comparison (Fig. 26) shows that standard DMD places discrete eigenvalues on the unit circle, while BOPDMD produces continuous eigenvalues exactly on the imaginary axis (zero real part by construction). Both identify the same set of harmonic frequencies. The amplitude spectra (Fig. 27) agree on the fundamental and first few harmonics; BOPDMD's spectrum is sparser due to its lower rank ($r = 11$) and the eigenvalue constraints.

Figure 26. DMD eigenvalues (left) vs. BOPDMD eigenvalues (right).
Figure 27. Amplitude spectra: DMD (left) vs. BOPDMD (right).
Both methods agree. For this well-behaved periodic flow, standard DMD and BOPDMD converge to the same physical picture. BOPDMD would become essential for noisier or higher-Re data where standard DMD eigenvalues scatter off the unit circle.

The 28 figures in this report trace a complete analytical arc. We began by visualising the raw data (§2), identified the intrinsic dimensionality via the singular spectrum (§3), extracted frequency-ranked modes and validated the Strouhal number (§4), demonstrated near-perfect reconstruction fidelity (§5), used selective reconstruction to isolate the physical role of each harmonic (§6), probed centreline wake physics through independent spectral methods (§7), compared the DMD basis with the energy-optimal POD basis (§8), and stress-tested robustness across ranks and algorithms (§9).

The central finding is that the $\mathrm{Re} = 100$ cylinder wake is an extremely low-dimensional dynamical system: five harmonics of a single frequency, encoded in ~11 complex degrees of freedom, capture 99.9% of an 89,351-dimensional vorticity field. This spectral compactness is the quantitative signature of a saturated global instability, and it is precisely the regime where DMD is most powerful.

The methodology extends naturally to more challenging flows — higher Reynolds numbers where the wake transitions to three-dimensional shedding, non-circular cylinders (Thompson et al. 2014), or transient regimes where BOPDMD's statistical robustness becomes essential. The framework established here provides the analytical template.
